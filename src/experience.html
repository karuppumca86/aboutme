Distributed Systems

Context

Led architecture for a high-throughput event processing system handling 50,000+ events per second across a globally distributed infrastructure. The system served as the backbone for real-time analytics, triggering automated responses and feeding downstream ML pipelines.

Constraints

  • 99.99% availability requirement with no more than 52 minutes of downtime per year
  • Sub-100ms end-to-end latency for critical event paths
  • Multi-region deployment with regulatory requirements for data residency
  • Legacy consumers that couldn't be modified, requiring backward compatibility

Key Decisions

  • Event sourcing with CQRS — Separated read and write paths to optimize for different access patterns and enable independent scaling
  • Partition-based architecture — Designed partitioning strategy around tenant ID to ensure strong ordering guarantees while enabling horizontal scaling
  • Circuit breaker at service boundaries — Implemented bulkheads and circuit breakers to prevent cascade failures across services

Outcomes

  • Achieved 99.995% availability over two years of operation
  • Reduced P99 latency from 2.3 seconds to 89 milliseconds
  • Enabled 10x throughput growth without significant re-architecture
  • Zero data loss incidents during three major regional outages

Lessons Learned

The biggest challenge wasn't the distributed systems complexity itself—it was helping the team develop intuition for distributed failure modes. We invested heavily in chaos engineering and game days, which paid dividends when real incidents occurred. Teams that had practiced failure scenarios recovered faster and with less stress.

Platform Engineering

Context

Built an internal developer platform from scratch for an organization of 200+ engineers across 30 teams. The goal was to reduce cognitive load on product teams by providing standardized paths for deployment, observability, and infrastructure provisioning.

Constraints

  • Existing heterogeneous tech stack (Java, Python, Node.js, Go) that couldn't be unified
  • Teams with varying levels of infrastructure expertise and autonomy needs
  • Strict security and compliance requirements (SOC2, GDPR) affecting deployment patterns
  • Need to support both greenfield and legacy applications during migration

Key Decisions

  • Golden paths, not golden cages — Provided opinionated defaults that covered 80% of use cases while allowing escape hatches for legitimate edge cases
  • Platform-as-product mindset — Treated internal teams as customers, with dedicated PM support, user research, and iterative improvement cycles
  • Self-service with guardrails — Enabled teams to provision resources independently while enforcing organization-wide policies through automation

Outcomes

  • Reduced time-to-production for new services from 2 weeks to 4 hours
  • Achieved 85% adoption across product teams within 18 months
  • Decreased infrastructure-related incidents by 60% through standardization
  • Enabled security team to implement org-wide policy changes in hours instead of months

Lessons Learned

The technical implementation was the easy part. The hard part was organizational change management—helping teams trust the platform, gathering feedback effectively, and navigating the politics of centralized vs. decentralized control. Success required as much time with stakeholders as with code.

Data Architecture

Context

Designed and led implementation of a unified data platform consolidating data from 40+ source systems. The platform needed to serve operational analytics, regulatory reporting, and machine learning workloads while maintaining data quality and lineage.

Constraints

  • Data sources ranged from modern APIs to mainframe batch exports and manual spreadsheets
  • Different business units owned different sources with varying data quality standards
  • Regulatory requirements mandated complete data lineage and audit trails
  • Existing BI tools and reports needed to continue functioning during migration

Key Decisions

  • Data mesh principles with pragmatic centralization — Domain teams owned their data products while a central team provided infrastructure and standards
  • Schema evolution strategy — Adopted Apache Avro with a schema registry to enable independent evolution of producers and consumers
  • Data quality as a first-class concern — Built automated quality checks into ingestion pipelines rather than treating quality as an afterthought

Outcomes

  • Consolidated from 12 data warehouses to a single unified platform
  • Reduced data pipeline failures from 15% to under 2%
  • Enabled self-service analytics for 500+ business users
  • Passed regulatory audit with zero findings on data lineage

Lessons Learned

Data projects live or die based on organizational buy-in, not technical excellence. Getting data owners to take responsibility for quality required creating incentive structures and making quality visible. The technical challenge of building the platform was dwarfed by the cultural challenge of improving data ownership.

System Evolution

Context

Led a multi-year modernization program for a 15-year-old monolithic system that processed $2B+ in annual transactions. The system was critical to business operations but was becoming increasingly difficult to modify, with deployment risk and cycle time both growing year over year.

Constraints

  • System couldn't be taken offline—business operated 24/7 across multiple time zones
  • Deep integration with partner systems through undocumented, fragile interfaces
  • Tribal knowledge concentrated in a small group of long-tenured developers
  • Multiple failed modernization attempts had created organizational skepticism

Key Decisions

  • Strangler fig pattern — Incrementally extracted functionality from the monolith rather than attempting a big-bang rewrite
  • Characterization testing — Built comprehensive test harnesses around existing behavior before attempting changes
  • Feature flags everywhere — Enabled gradual rollouts and instant rollbacks, reducing deployment risk to near-zero

Outcomes

  • Extracted 60% of functionality into modern services over 3 years
  • Reduced deployment frequency from monthly to daily
  • Cut average incident recovery time from 4 hours to 20 minutes
  • Zero business-impacting incidents during the entire migration

Lessons Learned

The strangler fig pattern works, but it requires patience and discipline that most organizations struggle to maintain. The key was celebrating incremental wins and making progress visible to leadership. We also learned that the best way to build trust after failed modernization attempts is to start with small, low-risk extractions and demonstrate success before tackling the hard parts.
